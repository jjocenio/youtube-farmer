## Thumbnail Strategy

### Visual Concept
The most visually charged moment in the Short is the journalist in his flat — morning light, plain envelope being opened, expression unreadable as he reads. Crop to 9:16 vertical: tight on his hands opening the envelope over a kitchen table, the leaflet inside partially visible, stamped "FREEDOM FOR AFRICA MOVEMENT." No return address visible. The viewer doesn't know who sent it. Neither did he. Text overlay makes the reveal.

### Fal.ai Prompt
Hyper-realistic 8K cinematic photograph, 720x1280 vertical canvas (9:16), Kodachrome 60s film grain, cinematic noir lighting. Vertical composition: overhead tight shot of hands opening a plain brown envelope on a dark wooden kitchen table, 1960s London morning — a propaganda leaflet partially emerging from the envelope, black-ink heading partially readable, no return address. Warm morning window light from upper left casting soft shadows across the table surface, a coffee mug blurred at the edge of the frame. Color grade: Kodachrome warm amber morning tones, slight desaturation, heavy grain. The envelope and leaflet are the only sharp elements in the frame. Atmosphere: ordinary moment, secret content — the mundane delivery of something sinister. Upper quarter of frame as dark ceiling negative space for text overlay.

### Text Overlay
**Suggested text:** BRITAIN INVENTED THAT ORGANIZATION

White stencil type in upper dark area. Works as a reveal — the viewer looks at the envelope, reads the text, and the cognitive dissonance drives them through the Short.

## Video Description

### Hook (first 150 characters — all that shows before "more")
For 29 years, Britain secretly ran 350 covert propaganda operations worldwide. The journalists publishing it had no idea.

### Full Description
The Information Research Department — the IRD — was Britain's Cold War propaganda machine. Born in 1948, it invented fake organizations, forged documents, and fed fabricated stories to newspapers across Africa, Asia, the Middle East, and Europe.

Its rule: nothing could ever identify Britain as the source. Over 100 journalists at major British newspapers received IRD material at their homes, in plain envelopes, marked personal.

The Soviets knew about it from the very first week. Guy Burgess — Cambridge spy, KGB agent since the 1930s — was seconded to the IRD in 1948 and immediately passed everything to Moscow.

Britain spent 29 years running propaganda operations with full Soviet visibility. The IRD was quietly shut down in 1977. No announcement. Files sealed for decades.
